Review of Edward Tufte’s “The Future of Data Analysis”

“What we see may not contain the answer.” This last quote from Edward Tufte’s talk on the Future of Data Analysis aptly sums up what he is attempting to convey to his audience. His talk composed of four main sections:

  1. Future excellence

  2. Getting it right in data analysis

  3. Confirmatory-unhacked vs. exploratory detective work

  4. The thinking eye

In his first section, Tufte discusses the potential society now has to capture large amounts of data and view it in a digestible way. He uses the example of measles vaccines to demonstrate how researchers were able to see 90 years of childhood disease rates to tie cases to the beginning of vaccination and conclude that vaccines had prevented 106 million cases of childhood disease and about 100 million deaths.

In his second section, Tufte discusses getting it right in data analysis. He discusses the fact that many research studies are false because they cannot be replicated and may have been overfitted, like being fitted in higher dimension space.

In his third section, Tufte discusses the common technique of exploring data and then guiding research by what is found. This is dangerous because it is tied to confirmation bias. He instead recommends thinking of questions ahead of time and creating content out of data rather than out of the analysis.

In his last section, Tufte brings together the previous topics: the ability to visualize big data, ensuring that data is correct/not overfitted/shown in the appropriate context, and guiding research with pre-specification rather than biased post-specification. All of these topics culminate in having a thinking eye, thinking critically about what you see and not assuming that the data will necessarily contain the answers (especially if we’re looking for certain answers, which will contribute to confirmation bias). It’s tempting to think that with so much data at hand, surely we can find some answers in the data but Tufte asks the audience to take a step back and assess the implications of eagerly attempting to gain answers from the data and humbly remembering in the pursuit of truth that “what we see may not contain the answer.”
